URS HOFMANN

Experiments on Canvas

About

I work on my canvases for many months or even years. Usually, when I start out, I have a plan but inevitably, as things progress, the plan changes or gets abandoned. Most often, after months and months and layer upon layer, I don't remember what the original plan was.

I work almost exclusively in oils on canvas although lately I’ve also worked with paper, oil pastels and glass. I only paint when the muse strikes. That very unreliable and fussy muse, my equally fussy and easily distracted self, the slow drying oil and the multitude of layers are the reasons for the extended time it takes me to finish a painting. I'm not in any hurry and more importantly, all the time that passes between inception and conclusion adds history, chronicle, several layers pigment and even dust (plus possibly an insect or two) which in turn all add to the texture and debth of the final "product". Like trees my paintings add growth rings; as a matter of fact, there is probably evidence of climate change buried deep on the layers of some of them.

I get inspired by all manner of things; I don't discriminate. The very strong perfect square is my favorite shape. I much prefer large formats because I like to get lost in a painting, its colors, textures, layers and patterns. The modest dimension of even my largest canvas is the consequence of the very small size of my studio (in the basement). "When I grow up" I will move into a warehouse and paint on a gigantic scale!

Rather than naming my finished works, I usually number them. I prefer to leave maximum flexibility for interpretation and exploration by the observer.

There are no messages or meanings, no fluffy stories and no cosmic connections to my paintings (even if there were I wouldn't tell). Since we have grown up together, I do however have an emotional connection to many of them. I build, construct, deconstruct and reconstruct a painting until I’m happy with it, until it "speaks" to me, until sitting with it, observing it and living with it for a while convinces me that I've done well.
